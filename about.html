About DOSA

The Distributed-power Open Standards Alliance (DOSA) is an open-standards consortium in the power electronics industry, focused on compatibility and standardization of DC-DC board-mounted power converters — notably the "Brick" modules and Point-of-Load (POL) converters used in distributed-power architectures. During the 2000s, the alliance shaped power-module form factors across telecommunications, data centers, and industrial applications.

Background & Objectives

DOSA was founded in a highly fragmented DC-DC converter market with the aim of "establishing customer interface standards early in the development cycle," so that power modules from different manufacturers would be compatible in package dimensions, pin definitions, and functional features. This compatibility made it easier for customers to qualify second sources and reduce design risk.

The alliance's standards covered non-isolated POL converters, Intermediate Bus Converters (IBCs) used in telecom and datacom, and a family of isolated Brick form factors — including 1/16, 1/8, 1/4, 1/2, full, and 1/32 Brick sizes.

Technical Contributions

The specifications developed by DOSA typically defined: package dimensions, pad and pin placements, functional pin assignments, basic electrical parameter ranges, and optional features such as enable, remote sense, and telemetry. Over time, the standards expanded to support higher power densities and digital power management — the third-generation high-density non-isolated MICRO POL standard, for example, introduced an I²C / PMBus digital communication interface.

Through these standards, system designers could freely switch between modules from different manufacturers — or migrate smoothly between product generations — without modifying PCB footprints or layouts. This was especially important for telecommunications, data centers, and industrial controls where power-supply reliability and maintainability are critical.

Impact & Current Status

Around 2010, DOSA attracted more than a dozen major board-mounted power manufacturers — including Murata Power Solutions, Delta Electronics, and TDK-Lambda — resulting in multi-source compatible implementations for most common Brick modules, essentially unifying the physical specifications across the industry.

As digital power management and newer alliances emerged (such as organizations built around PMBus with a stronger emphasis on digital interoperability), DOSA's role gradually diminished. It is now regarded as one of the earlier standardization efforts that primarily addressed "physical compatibility" rather than full "digital interoperability."
